Free Will

The concept of free will is a very interesting topic to tackle with. I question everything that is curious to me and free will is one of them. After many hours of reading and listening from various ministers and scientists addressing and pondering about free will, I came to the conclusion that free will indeed is an illusion and I am yet to find a person capable of defining it coherently, let alone present a compelling argument that it exists. Here in this article, we will take a look at how Mr. Controversy X deals with the question of free will.

According to Mr. Controversy X, free will is "something done without compulsion and with willingness". A not-so-fine definition but we can still use this one to examine his claims. He also mentioned Encarta's definition as "done voluntarily rather than by compulsion". When he started quoting biblical passages, he seems to contradict his own definition by stating that our free will is a free choice to either do what God wants us to do for His delight or be "destroyed", or punished in some biblically horrible ways. If you are compelled to do something for being threatened with Hell and destruction, how is that a free will? If by free will we choose to do what we want instead of what God wants us to do, why it is called "rebelling"?

That is the essence of "free will" according to Mr. Controversy X. Free will is a free choice to do what God wants us to do (praising and stroking His ego, including and not limited to giving money to His ministers), or burn in the Lake of Fire. Does that sound like free will to you? It contradicts his own definition of free will, which is something done without compulsion and with willingness. To threaten someone with everlasting life in agony and suffering is not a free choice, my friend. It is extortion. I assume Alsie616 is smart enough to understand this simple distinction.

Mr. Controversy X may argue that God is being pious and this free choice "is the most loving and compassionate act" He did. If you believe that is so, then picture these two examples as these are based on the logic that he used:

A man came to a woman in the bar and told her he loves her very much. With sincerity, he added that she has the free will to choose to either love him back or not. But if she doesn't love him back, the man will torture her to death.

Is that really free will? Is she really free to choose, without compulsion in any way? According to Mr. Controversy X's logic, the man is being loving and compassionate for giving the woman free will and if she chooses other than what he likes, it will be her own fault for being tortured to death.

Another example. And this will be the case if Mr. Controversy X's witty logic of free will is correct.

Imagine there's a robber who came to Elisha and he held him up, telling him at gunpoint to give him his money. Well, with love and compassion, the robber gives Elisha the free will to choose: either he gives him his money or be shot in the head. Based on Mr. Controversy X's logic, if Elisha refuses to give up his money to the robber and got shot, it will be Elisha's fault. Elisha was given a free will and he freely chooses to die. Not the robber's fault.

These scenarios are exactly what Mr. Controversy X's idea of free will is. God gave us the free will to either do whatever He likes, or suffer in an excruciating way for everlasting. That is how his idea of free will works, so absurd and nonsensical.

Using Mr. Controversy X's definition (free will is something done without compulsion and with willingness), what actions that you do freely in your daily lives, that is, without compulsion and with willingness? You wake up because you are compelled to by your body's internal clock, or the noise, or the alarm that you set up last night, or the emergency call that your phone received. You eat because hunger compelled you to replenish. You read this post because you were compelled by curiosity or your thirst for knowledge. If you choose to close your browser just to test or try to prove your free will of having done something without compulsion, then guess what? You still are being compelled to do just that by the personality that was developed since you were a child through experiences in those sets of circumstances that you did not orchestrate, and the books that you read that were written by someone else, the lessons that you learned from others, and things that you observed from your surroundings. Everything that formed your personality that has this tendency to close a browser instead of raging in the streets when compelled by the thought to test yourself for free will when you read about something that tells you there is no such thing. You do things because it is either you were compelled to or because you want to. And what you want is not your own will either. You did not 'will' to want ice cream (or any other things for that matter). You just happen to like a hazelnut-flavoured ice cream because the first time you eat hazelnut ice cream, your brain brewed a concoction of chemicals that made you happy. A process that you have no control of. And you just remembered it, not on your own will. The thought just occurred.

Here is a very simple example:
A. A man is being loved by two women B and C. He can freely choose from the two; but he chose B. Is it not free will?

When the man chose Lady B, something must have compelled him to choose the first from the two. Think about it, there are more or less than three billion women in the world, isn't he restricted to choose from just between these two and can't freely choose among all the women in the world? And talking about choosing between two women, B and C, he'll choose which he likes, which is B. And we are back again at the starting point, the illusion of free will. The man will be compelled to choose woman B because he likes her more than woman A. The man didn't decide what are his likes and dislikes. Do you think you choose what you like and dislike? So wrapping up the answer to this question, the man is compelled to choose between these two women, B and C, based on his preferences he himself didn't decide. He was compelled to chose between the two because of some circumstances he has no control of. Even the reason why he is in the position of choosing one woman is also a compulsion. Maybe the need for a wife, the pressure of the parents' demand, or the society's expectation of him to have a family. All these compels him to choose. If he did it with all these things in response to compulsions, then it is not free will by definition.

B. In another case, he chooses both and made them both suffer at the end. Of course, suffering comes if you do something with your own free will which is against the will of God.

Mr. Controversy X is being contradictory again, as always. The bishop is being compelled (this time by the Bible) to be the husband of one wife. He must be blameless, vigilant, etc. Again, if he is being compelled to do and be one of these things, then contrary to what Mr. Controversy X says, the bishop (or any man for that matter) has no free will, again, by definition.

To understand the deeper part of it, we can just look on our being as an individual. In our bodies, there are what science calls as muscles acting voluntarily and involuntarily. The heart is one of them. It beats and pumps blood throughout the body unceasingly throughout one’s lifetime without the control of man himself; while voluntary muscles moves are controlled by the mind.

I didn't include the texts from Mr. Controversy X's blog post after this part because it is irrelevant to the issue at hand. Again, if everything we do is being compelled and are not our will, then by definition we don't have free will.


Conclusions

So to wrap things up, Mr. Controversy X's idea of free will is self-defeating and illogical. He defined free will as something done without compulsion and with willingness. He said angels have free will and the proof of that is they were able to leave their posts and rebel against God. If choosing to do other than what God wants is called rebelling, it is not "free choice" then, now is it? Otherwise, it would be contradictory in terms. Mr. Controversy X argues that we humans were given free will to either do what God wants us to do or suffer His wrath. That contradicts his definition of free will, which says that it is something done without compulsion and with willingness. To threaten a person to do your bidding is not free will. It is extortion. Mr. Controversy X has no convincing argument about free will, but bogus, contradictory statements and quotations. No sign of wit whatsoever.
